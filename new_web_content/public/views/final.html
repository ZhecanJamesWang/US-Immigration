A Shift of Opinions: Immigration and the United States
This election cycle has once again cast a limelight upon the issue of immigration in the United States, referencing both legal and undocumented immigrants, and even refugees fleeing war-torn countries. It’s amazing that we take the rhetoric that some politicians display as the norm nowadays, since 30 years ago, this debate was aired between George H. W. Bush and Ronald Reagan.
Immigration Fluctuation
It's not only rhetoric that has varied greatly over the years. Immigration rates themselves have fluctuated greatly in recent years, varying by over 150 million in the past thirty years alone.

When did the United States' attitude towards immigrants become so polarized, and even hostile at times? It's necessary to investigate this question through multiple routes.
Routes of Investigation
One is through the Congressional Bills which have pertained to immigration. They provided us with the legislative view of the government’s attitude towards immigration.

Second was through archived New York Times articles which contained the word immigration. This let us view immigration through the lens of the general public and (tending liberal) media.

Finally, we found very interesting significant statistics published by the Department of Homeland Security, which gave us a perspective on the executive actions of the government.
Taking a closer look into Bills
We decided to zoom in on the range of years between 1990 and 2005, as Congress data prior to those dates appeared to be quite deprecated.
Unique Bills
Although there were numerous amendments to bills, we were able to extract the first version of those bills related to immigration, and collect 114 unique documents to process.
What are they about
After obtaining the content of the bills we collected their top subject terms via TF-IDF and also by consulting with the words listed in the dataset that matched our predicions.

We were very interested in exploring the topic of Economics and Finance, as it was the most frequent one.
The Democratic "Yes"
We were also very interested in seeing how the different parties reacted to bills about Immigration and the Economy. No party was particularly inclined to vote potively about a bill, and significant noise existed in the "yes" voting percentage of this party, as shown on the graph on the right.
Bills and 1997
After thorough research and analysis performed, as documented here, we came to the conlusion that the year 1997 stood out as a year with multiple bills targetted towards the US economy and the international affair matters that the country was involved at the time, i.e. Cuba.

We then wanted to see whether our other data sets could support or add on to this discovery.
Articles and Clusters

More detailed analysis result about this part can be found here We scraped the New York Times archives over the same range of years, totalling around 15,000 articles. We used TF-IDF to analyze all the articles and a K-means clustering algorithm to create 5 clusters out of our data.

On the right, we've grouped the articles together into 100 bins among the 15000 articles, so each circle represents around 150 articles. Each circle is originally a multi-dimensional feature vector and should be graphed in a high dimensional space, but here we've projected them into a two dimensional plane by using Multidimensional Scaling (reduction). As you may observe, the circles in a cluster tend to be closer together in spatial distance. Also, the year of circles in a cluster also tend to be closer in chronological distance, and the clusters’ keywords are very distinct from each other. It appears to be a reasonable clustering of our data.

Analyzing the Groupings

The overall clusters distribution across years. Among the five clusters, there are two which may be more interesting. The first one is the “kosovo” (second in order) cluster that tends to center around 1999. The background context can be referred to the Kosovo War, Kosovo Albanians and so on. The second one is the “cubans” (fourth) cluster that tends to center around 2000. The background context can be referred to the Elian Gonzalez custody battle, former Cuban castaway Elián González and so on. The other clusters tend to be more general comparatively.

Analyzing the Words

After clustering, we gathered the central words from each cluster along with their importance score from the TF-IDF. We used both histograms and word clouds to visualize the words, to glean information from them. On the right, we can see that for the second cluster, “kosovo” is the most significant word, accompanied by several other words pertaining to Yugoslavia: “albanian”, “serbian”, etc.

Seeing What's Important

Dancing in the Clouds

Articles and 1999

The other clusters also point to certain other subjects and events, such as the custody battle for Elian Gonzalez, a cuban child. The "Kosovo" cluster, however, more strongly points to a noteworthy event: the Kosovo War of 1998 and '99.

Naturalizations in the U.S.
The final realm of data we explored was meant to represent the executive side of government. We gathered copious amounts of data from various metrics published by the Department of Homeland Security in order to attempt and construct this final perspective on immigration.

In this graph, we see petitions for naturalization in yellow, naturalizations in green, and rejections in red. We can already begin to see an interesting upwards spike in 1990, peaking at 1996.
Actions of the DHS
Contained in our data were copious metrics such as immigration, denied entries, border patrol actions, deportations, and prosecutions regarding immigration. By analyzing more of the datasets, we found that our initial interest in 1990 and 1996 is not unfounded. 1996 seems to be a year that immigration trends declined, and negative attitudes and actions towards immigrants, such as years of imprisonment sentenced regarding immigration violations began to rise.
Immigration and 1996
The upward trend in naturalization we found beginning in 1989 is very likely a result of the dissolution of the soviet bloc, which lead to an increase in immigrants to the United States, which is supported by the fact that approximately four times more European immigrants entered the United States in 1989 than in 1996.

However this event is external to the U.S, which makes the year 1996 our focus. This was the beginning of Clinton's second term, when there was bipartisan support for a very strong "war aginst crime" movement in Congress. This connects to our legislative data which didn't show one party voting far more strongly for immigration bills.
