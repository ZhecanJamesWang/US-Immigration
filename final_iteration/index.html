A Shift of Opinions: Immigration and the United States
This election cycle has once again cast a limelight upon the issue of immigration in the United States, referencing both legal and undocumented immigrants, and even refugees fleeing war-torn countries. It’s amazing that we take the rhetoric that some politicians display as the norm nowadays, since 30 years ago, this debate was aired.
Immigration Fluctuation
It's not only rhetoric that has varied greatly over the years. Immigration rates themselves have fluctuated greatly in recent years, varying by over 150 million in the past thirty years alone.

When did the United States' attitude towards immigrants become so polarized, and sometimes hostile? It's necessary to investigate this question through multiple routes.
Routes of Investigation
One is through the Congressional Bills which pertained to immigration. This provided us with a view of the legislative view of the government’s attitude towards immigration.

Second was through archived New York Times articles which contained the word immigration. This let us view immigration through the lens of the general public and (in this case mostly liberal) media.

Finally, we found very interesting significant statistics published by the Department of Homeland Security. This gave us a perspective on the executive actions of the government.
Taking a closer look into Bills
We decided to zoom into the early 1990's till 2005, as Congress data prior to those dates appear deprecated.
Unique Bills
Although there were numerous amendments to bills, we were able to extract the first version of those bills related to immigration and, hence, collect 114 unique documents to process.
What are they about
After obtaining the content of the bills we collected their top_subject terms via TF-IDF and also by consulting with the words listed in the dataset that matched our predicions.

We were very interested in exploring the topic of Economics and Finance, as it was the most frequent one.
The Democratic "Yes"
We were also very interested in seeing how democratic parties reacted to such bills about Immigration and the Economy. No party was particularly inclined to vote potively about a bill, and significant noise existed in the "yes" voting percentage of this party, as shown on the graph on the right.
Bills and 1997
After thorough research and analysis performed, as documented here, we came to the conlusion that the year 1997 stood out as a year with multiple bills targetted towards the US economy and the international affair matters that the country was involved at the time, i.e. Cuba.

We then wanted to see whether the press had a similar say to this scene.
